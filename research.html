Spatiotemporal Physics-Informed Machine Learning Model for Weather Prediction in Uganda

Introduction

Accurate weather forecasting is critical for building societal resilience, particularly in regions like Uganda where agriculture, disaster management, and water resource planning heavily depend on timely, localized forecasts. However, traditional Numerical Weather Prediction (NWP) models, while foundational, face major limitations: they are computationally intensive, sensitive to initial conditions, and often perform poorly at local or micro-scales due to sparse observational networks and coarse model resolutions.

Research Motivation

Purely data-driven Machine Learning (ML) models offer an alternative by uncovering complex spatiotemporal patterns in large datasets, but they often lack physical consistency, leading to unrealistic or unstable predictions — especially at finer spatial scales. This gap is particularly critical for micro-scale phenomena like local thunderstorms, heavy rainfall clusters, lake-effect storms, and sudden temperature shifts, which have high impact yet are difficult to predict with existing models.

Motivated by these challenges, this research aims to develop a Spatiotemporal Physics-Informed Machine Learning (PINN) model that combines the flexibility of deep learning (CNNs, LSTMs, GNNs, FNOs) with embedded physical laws (Navier-Stokes equations, energy conservation, moisture balance) to produce physically consistent, high-resolution, micro-scale weather forecasts. By leveraging satellite observations, reanalysis datasets, and in-situ measurements, the model will improve forecast reliability and interpretability, even in data-scarce environments.

Ultimately, this research is driven by the need to advance next-generation AI systems capable of supporting localized decision-making in agriculture, disaster early warning, and climate resilience planning — delivering actionable, precise, and physically trustworthy weather insights at the community level.
